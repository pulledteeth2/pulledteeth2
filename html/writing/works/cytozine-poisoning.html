June 2022

[GUARD] V-6723909 WAS INVOLVED IN AN ACCIDENT ON NAVATROLS BASE 3. THE FOLLOWING ARE THE NAVATROLS GENERAL ACCESS MEMORY FILES OF [GUARD] V-6723909. NAVATROLS HAS THE RIGHT TO ALTER, REDACT, AND WITHHOLD ANY SENSITIVE INFORMATION WITHIN THE MEMORY FILES OF [GUARD] V-6723909.

June 2002

Whatever it was, it made me choke. I could hear my mom yelling at my dad over my coughing. He tried to assure her that “it was fine” and “most [I’ll] get is a headache.” And he was correct in saying that my head would almost permanently hurt, but the damage from the Cytozine exposure was far greater than a headache. While it’s not deadly for humans, Cytozine, at the time, was still too unstable to expose to children under 17. My dad, being the model Hensodyne employee he was, offered me for Exposure Testing.

The side effect list was played back too quickly for me to understand. It didn’t matter whether or not I agreed anyway. My dad signed off, and I was shoved into a white room, 5 meters on all sides, with a one-way mirror on one wall. This was the standard for all holding rooms at Hensodyne. Every day, a Testing Officer would come in, put an anesthesia mask over my face, and leave it there for a few hours. I wasn’t allowed to speak, or the Testing Officer would pump more Cytozine through the mask. It lasted for 25 days. I don’t remember how many kids were involved in Exposure Testing, but I do remember that one of them died. I remember that day we were released, watching the mother fall to her knees and weep. The father, the Hensodyne employee, didn’t even look at her, didn’t even look sad. He nodded along as the Testing Officer explained the accident, almost like it was just another day for him. Almost like he hadn’t just killed his own child. I remember looking up at my dad, only to see he had the same reaction. I told myself it was different, that he was allowed to react nonchalantly because I didn’t die, that, at most, I would only have nightmares, nosebleeds, and ear infections for the rest of my life, but something about his lack of care in that moment bothered me. He wasn’t even happy to see me. He didn’t even look at me.

May 2004

“Locked out again?”

His voice startled me, even though I knew it was him. It was always him. When [DEMO] C-2234599 first moved into the apartment across from ours, he had just ignored me, but I guess he started feeling bad for me after a while. Eventually, he would sit next to me in the hallway and help me do my homework. He wasn’t very good at math, which is what I needed the most help with, but he tried and I was thankful.

“What do you do for work? You always smell like bleach.”

“I work for Navatrols. Base 1.”

“In D.C.?”

“Yeah.”

“Oh…”

“Something wrong with that?”

“No. That’s kinda far though. Why not live there? It’s probably cheaper.”

“My mom doesn’t wanna live in D.C. She lives with me, so I stay here for her.”

At some point he started inviting me inside so I could do my work at a table instead of the floor. The apartment had a strange setup. The line between I met his mom and his cat, Mouse. His mom always made sure I ate when I was there and she was much better at algebra than I expected. She told me she also worked at Base 4 before she retired. She never explicitly told me what she did there, but I assumed it had something to do with math. Maybe she was a missile calculator?

I was thankful that neither of them ever asked why I didn’t have a key to my apartment. My parents never gave me a reason. It was always just “No”. I guess they thought I would lose it or something. I didn’t really lose or misplace things growing up though, so I didn’t understand. I remember putting it on my birthday lists and Christmas lists every year. The other kids at school had their own keys—why couldn’t I have one too? No matter how many times I asked though, the answer was always no. But after a while, it didn’t really matter to me any more. Not having a key gave me a reason to spend more time with [DEMO] C-2234599.

There was one day my parents didn’t come home. [DEMO] C-2234599 let me sleep on the couch with Mouse. Apparently, I got up in the middle of the night, went into his room, and calmly told him that I couldn’t see anything but it felt like my skin was slowly being ripped off of my body, and that there was a weird feeling spreading through me and it hurt. I don’t remember that, but I do remember waking up next to him. He tried to get out of bed without waking me up, I tried clinging onto him to keep him there. He was so gentle when he told me to go back to sleep. I wish I could’ve kept him there longer. I would’ve given anything for five more minutes.

June 2007

“I woke up in the grass again this morning.”

He didn’t answer. I didn’t think he would answer. What is he thinking? Is he mad? Did I say something wrong? Should I have even called? Is he too busy to talk to me? Is he mad? But when has he ever been mad at me? He’s never hurt my feelings. Even when he tried to throw me a surprise party by letting me think he had forgotten my birthday. We had lemon cake that year. I think his mom made it. She’s always been really nice to me too.

“How far this time?”

[DEMO] C-2234599 sounded more bored than anything else, I could at least tell that. But maybe it was just because of the drive. People get bored when they drive, right? Especially when it’s a route they take a lot. That must be it. He couldn’t be mad at me. And it’s early, so he’s probably still tired. Maybe he isn’t actually bored, but just tired? He still likes me. He has to. He’s just tired.

I answered his question the best I could, but I was met with silence again. I hope he didn’t fall asleep while driving. I stood up, brushed the grass off of my legs, and started walking. Not home, in the opposite direction. Towards the road, where I could pretend that I was in his passenger seat again. I didn’t pay much attention to the cars weaving around me while I crossed the street, not until I was nearly flattened by a red Honda Accord.

“I think I just saw your car.”

“Liar. There’s no way you made it this far in one night.”

“You’re right. It just looks like yours.”

I couldn’t think of anything else to say. I just wanted him to keep talking to me. When I was still waking up in Asbury Corner, he could walk me home and tell me a story. He told me about Navatrols’ newest project and all the amazing things it would do once developed. All the people it might save. He shared his frustration with development. I didn’t really understand. [DEMO] C-2234599 said he can’t tell me more, that I knew too much already. My sleepwalking had gotten so bad that I didn’t wake up in Asbury Corner anymore, so he didn’t have time to walk me home.

“Hey, I’m about to go in. I’ll talk to you later, alright?”

And I knew he wouldn’t talk to me later, unless later meant tomorrow. He didn’t give me time to convince him to stay on the phone. He did tell me once that he doesn’t like talking and driving. That must be it. He can’t be mad at me. I kept walking, I think subconsciously, I knew where I was going. When I got there, I jumped Pleasant Pond’s fence and stumbled my way over to the swings. Whenever I was in the park alone, I would lay across two swings and use them like a hammock. And I slept there until the park manager hosed me down.

I spent my time at home waiting for the day to pass, until I would inevitably fall asleep again and wake up in some strange new place. I never once remembered my dreams. No one ever tried to stop me. No one ever cared, no matter how much they said they did. I remember the first time I woke up outside. [DEMO] C-2234599 sprinted to get me. He picked me up, carried me home, told me I’d be okay. He was so soft and I, for once, felt safe. I could’ve spent an eternity being carried. And he kept checking on me throughout the day. I went to his house for dinner. His mom made spaghetti. I don’t really like spaghetti but I didn’t tell her that. It was good anyway.

I woke up on a bench in Pleasant Pond the next morning. I was wet. Not because of a park manager, but because it rained. And it was still raining when I woke up. I didn’t call [DEMO] C-2234599 this time. It was early enough that he probably hadn’t left for work yet but I didn’t call him. I didn’t go home. I didn’t want to leave Pleasant Pond. So I wandered. I hadn’t seen most of Pleasant Pond since I was 13, before I moved away. My dad had gotten a new position with Hensodyne, and, for whatever reason, my mom decided we’d move with him. He didn’t like Pleasant Pond. He swore that he wasn’t scared of the cemetery half, but it was more than obvious that he wasn’t a fan, so I was never able to explore.

Unsurprisingly, that’s where I found myself that day, walking up and down and around the rows of headstones. I read the names of the people buried there and the years they died. I looked for any that died in 1990. I thought I might’ve been a reincarnation of them. All of them combined, their personalities blending and blurring together to make up my own. I looked for the years my parents were born too, maybe to finally understand why they are the way they are. And I looked for [DEMO] C-2234599, maybe to find who he may have been in his past life. I stopped walking when I found a mausoleum with 1978 engraved into it. One of many Grand Asset tombs, but the prettiest for sure. Conrad Roswell.

September 2009

The sound of the doorbell made me flinch. I couldn’t see from where I was, but I heard the door to the apartment open, and [DEMO] C-2234599 greeting whoever was there. I heard them shuffling through the apartment, eventually sitting down and clattering silverware against plates. It didn’t take them long after their meal to make their way to the bedroom, and from my spot in the closet, I could see it all. I didn’t do anything weird, I didn’t want her to catch me. I was just dying to be her.

“What are you doing in my apartment?”

“I just wanted to see you.”

“How did you get in my apartment!?”

“Please. I just wanna talk to you.”

“Get out!”

“Please? It’s my birthday.”

But [DEMO] C-2234599 didn’t listen. So I sat outside and waited. It was raining. Why did he leave me in the cold rain? On my birthday? I just wanted to talk to him. I don’t remember how long I waited. I fell asleep at some point. When I woke up, I was in Pleasant Pond, lying in the grass next to Conrad Roswell’s mausoleum. Exactly where I wanted to be. It reminded me of [DEMO] C-2234599. I was glad to just have someone there at my side. I stared at that 1978 and wondered how he died. He was too young to have died from old age, he was only 59. I had considered trying to research him, but I liked the mystery at the time. Maybe he was hit by a bus. Maybe he worked at Navatrols and got in an accident. Maybe he worked at Hensodyne and got in an accident. I don’t remember how long I stayed there. A police officer woke me up. He said something about a fine. I don’t remember what I told him. I remember the rain hitting my face. He didn’t have an umbrella. I went with him to the car. He talked a lot but he promised I wasn’t being arrested. We got to the station at some point and he gave me water. He wanted me to call my mom but I wasn’t ready to go home yet. He was nice and his voice didn’t annoy me, so I let him keep talking about his wife and kids. I didn’t care very much about them. He has a son my age.

After three times of asking if I knew my mom’s number (followed by some unrelated tangent), I did eventually call my mom. She wasn’t angry on the ride home. Or, rather, she didn’t yell at me. She didn’t say anything. I don’t mind silence.

I was in my room on the floor, on my back, specifically. I painted my ceiling for the first time when I was 15. It was a night sky with 217 stars. My dad hit me so hard that night, I almost cried. My mom let me keep the paint though. I repainted it last year, and, at that moment, staring at the portrait of [DEMO] C-2234599 on my ceiling, I found myself being more angry than usual. Or, maybe not angry, but something more than usual. I got undressed.

December 2009

I found myself at the library searching for Conrad Roswell’s obituary. I lied and told the librarian that he was a relative of mine so she could help me access his Memory Files. She guided me back to one of the study rooms and I watched her override the Linktyzen restrictions. Once we found Conrad Roswell, she left me alone, wishing me good luck or whatever.

Conrad, as I suspected, was a Hensodyne employee. I could tell because the type of mausoleum he had is reserved for Grand Assets of Arms. I sat there for hours reading through his Memory Files. Maybe I needed him. Maybe I wanted him to care about me. If I read through his Memory Files, that must make us closer, right? That makes him real.

And real, to me, he was. He discovered Cytozine back in the 60s. His method is redacted, but I don’t think I would’ve read it anyway. His findings were also mostly redacted, but I was able to deduce most of it. He called everyone with Cytozine an “aliysies”. Essentially, each aliysies has its own Cytozine pattern, which determines the sorts of abilities it has. In theory, then, an aliysies could be incredibly weak. Then why are the Grand Arms trying to make us believe that all aliysies are a threat? Conrad’s Memory Files also reveal that Hensodyne was first to begin Aliysies Conversion, but was never successful.

August 2014

There was a boy in the cemetery. He had a small cardboard box held together by a rubber band. I asked him what was in the box, but I don’t think he understood English. He handed me the box. It had a dead rat inside. I almost dropped it in disgust, but I helped him find a place in the cemetery to bury it anyway. He didn’t seem all that sad for the rat, in fact, he acted more like this was his job. He didn’t stay around long.

I saw him again a few times, each time with a dead rat, and each time, I helped him dig a spot in the cemetery to bury them. Someone called the police once. I didn’t necessarily feel bad for him. Burying dead street rats is a strange thing. Is he killing the rats himself? Where is he getting the cardboard?

“Why do you do this?”

Of course, he didn’t answer. I didn’t think he’d answer. I tried asking him a few times, but he ignored me completely. So I started telling him whatever I felt like. My parents’ divorce. Losing my job (again). I even showed him Conrad Roswell, not that the boy was all that impressed. But I wanted to learn more about him anyway. One day I brought him an English picture dictionary to try and make him talk to me, at least through pictures. He didn’t engage. He had on a new pair of shoes that day. Well, the shoes weren’t new, but they were different from the shoes he usually wore.

“Where did you get your shoes? Did you steal them?”

Even though he ignored me, I had still hoped he would tell me. But I saw something in his face. It was small, but it was there. Confusion. He was confused by what I said, so he must have some sort of understanding of what I’m saying. He’s never looked confused when I talk to him. So he knows what I’m saying. He’s known English this whole time and he’s just been fucking ignoring me?

“You fucking lied to me! You know what I’m saying! Talk to me!”

Something about his face just called me to violence. I was tired of being ignored. I wanted him to care about me. I wanted him to care about the things I cared about. I grabbed the boy by his hair and dragged him to Conrad’s grave. I wanted Conrad to see this, I knew he would care. I started shaking the boy but he didn’t say anything. He whimpered and tried punching me, so I threw him down. Rather than crawling away like I expected him to do, he lunged at me and bit my face. He didn’t stop there. I could feel every individual tooth digging into my cheek. And, despite being quite small, he pinned me to the ground. He thrashed, ripping my flesh from my cheek, and I cried out for help, but he kicked my ribs to silence me. I guess at some point he had had enough, and he scurried off somewhere else in the park. I didn’t see where he went. I didn’t bother getting up. Not right away, anyway. I didn’t even know what to do with myself. Of course, I should’ve called the police, but something was stopping me from doing so. Not because I cared about what may have happened to the boy, but I guess the shock of being bitten by a feral child locked me in place. I stayed there with Conrad, hoping he would tell me what to do next. When I got no answer I stood up and walked home, holding my bleeding cheek the entire way.

September 2019

I didn’t start working with Navatrols because I wanted to. I wanted to see [DEMO] C-2234599 again, but we weren’t assigned to the same base. I felt sick when I was told I’d be working in Walkertrap, sick when I saw the flight confirmation, sick when I packed my things, sick when I got on the plane, sick all 24 hours of my flight, sick when I landed, sick when I got to my lodging, sick on my first day. I knew this was where the dog was being developed and I, momentarily, was excited to finally see it. But apparently, it was gone. They showed me the room it would’ve been kept in if it was still in their possession; 5 meters on all sides, painted white with a one-way mirror on one wall. It looked like it hadn’t been cleaned in a long time—dried blood on the cracked floor in the corner. Boss told me not to worry about it.

“It’s got a 300 meter radius. Pretty powerful thing.”

I didn’t know what that meant and I didn’t really care enough to ask. But I would continually get small pieces of information about the dog. Some of my coworkers had been working with Navatrols when the dog (though, it’s official name is K93-M52, as I later learned) was being developed. They told me that it would’ve been Navatrols’ greatest asset, and the company could finally outperform Hensodyne. But somewhere in the transfer process from Walkertrap to D.C., the dog was lost. My boss never confirmed it, but I assumed whoever was in charge of handling the dog was neutralized.

Despite the change in scenery, like the Health Administrator suggested, my sleepwalking persisted. But since I was sleeping during the day, I at least didn’t have to wait for morning for someone to catch me. In my lodging were four other Navatrols employees. I didn’t particularly get along with them, but at least one of them would tase me awake when they saw me sleepwalking.

My job was quite boring. I just had to sit in a tower and make sure no one got close. From my post, I could see most of the base’s testing grounds, some of the city’s skyline, and, if I squinted hard enough and the weather was alright, I could see the water. I think the last time I went swimming was with [DEMO] C-2234599. I was 16 then, he had brought me with him for the summer in D.C. We went hiking and I fell into the river. He tried his best not to laugh at me, but he couldn’t help it. So, to make me feel better, he got in the river too. He walked back to the car shirtless. I committed the image to memory, so much so that I would paint it on my ceiling.

November 2021

Base 3 felt alive for the first time when we learned that the dog was located and being transported to us. The whole day felt like the day before Christmas, where you know you’re about to open the exact present you wanted. Everyone was on base that warm late spring day, not even to work, but to see the glorious machine resume its position on our grounds. I was lucky enough to get a spot in the Observation Station.

I tried my best to look as excited as everyone else, but I couldn't help but be disgusted when I saw one of the Handlers throwing the young man into the room. That’s the “dog”? This boy is supposed to be Navatrols’ greatest asset? He looked no older than 25. His gunsmoke gray Navatrols branded shirt made his bronze skin look sickly. I watched as another Handler sharpened the prongs of a large metal collar, bent down to lift the boy by his hair, then forced the prongs into the boy’s neck. His unconscious body stiffened upon puncture, and I could barely make out some tattoo being ruined by the spike. He had another on his hand, a spindly redback spider, the red ink gleamed brighter than the blood trickling down his neck. He had more on his legs, and I couldn’t help but let my mind come up with what others may lie beneath his clothes. If I had seen him on the street, I would’ve thought nothing special, aside from just seeing him as an attractive young man. But on Base 3, he was a pathetic dog. He was supposed to be a dangerous aliysies that would lead to the death of far too many people. He was supposed to be a monster and I was supposed to hate him. But as I watched the Handlers ensure his collar was properly chained to the wall, I couldn’t help but imagine what his life was like before this. Was he in college? Did he have a girlfriend? What did his friends think of him? Did he have parents? Did they love him? Something about him was so familiar, but I couldn’t place it. He didn’t have the kind of face that you could forget. In his unconscious state, he looked too innocent to be a living military grade weapon. A newborn puppy, rather than a vicious canine. I wanted to touch his face. I wanted to run my fingers through his hair. I wanted to wake him up and carry him back to my lodging. I wanted to hear his voice. I wanted to see the sun on his face.

March 2022

From my post in Watch Tower 9, I watched K93-M52 being tested outside in the Wide Grounds. Every ten minutes or so, I’d see the Demo being clouded by a wall of black smoke (or, what looked like smoke?), and then, after a few seconds, the smoke would dissipate, and the Demo would be writhing on the ground. The dog just stood there, obeying orders, waiting to be let back inside, and looking pretty pathetic doing so. After a few minutes of taunting, the Handler pulled the dog along by its collar, escorting it inside.

And, for whatever reason, on my break, I found myself standing in the Observation Station, watching that shifts’ Handlers monitor the dog via surveillance. Something about the way the dog seemed to shrink away in its corner almost made me… care? I asked one of the Handlers to unlock the Cage. At first there was some resistance (what purpose does a Guard have with the dog?), but apparently, I was convincing enough, and they let me in the dog’s room.

“I saw you training.”

Of course, the dog did not respond. At first, I didn’t think it could speak, but Handlers later told me otherwise: Any noise from the dog awarded 15 Amps through its prong collar, directly into its neck. They tried explaining exactly which muscles, but I didn’t care enough to listen and learn. The dog at least gave me the respect of lifting its vengeful eyes to meet mine. It was like staring into a black hole, with the addition of nearly dry blood crusting around its right eye. There was something almost human there… I crouched down in front of it, so we were eye level.

“Out of everyone, why you?”

I had read the dog’s file, both the capture from 2007 and the recapture from November. Why did Navatrols send Base 3 soldiers into Tasmania, neutralize half of a town, and kidnap a 7 year old boy? To then turn him into this… thing. This pathetic machine that threatens to rip my limbs from my body, to stop my organs with just a look, to turn me inside out. And it was there that I stopped caring. This dog is not that boy. The boy is dead, and has been for years now. I rose to my feet, and, rather than leaving the Cage, I kicked the dog on the open bruise above its eye. And I kept kicking. I didn’t know why, it just felt like an urge. Some of the other Handlers tried to explain it to me; something about the dog just called them to beat it. Maybe it still looked too human. Maybe it still thought it could be human. Maybe all aliysies are that way. Did Hensodyne redact that from Conrad’s Memory Files? I believe we were just trying to help it see. The dog cannot be human. There’s nothing human about it beyond its flesh. I wiped its blood from my boot using its shirt, then I went back to my post.

June 2022

I was at my lodging when I heard the announcement that K93-M52 would be neutralized. I was surprised, because it had only returned to Navatrols’ possession just seven months before. What could’ve gone so terribly wrong in that short amount of time? I decided that, rather than sleep, I would arrive early at the base so I could finally see it. The base was holding a “watch party” of sorts—most of my coworkers, if not scheduled at the time, were there.

I was lucky enough to get a spot in the Observation Station. It must’ve sensed that there were more people watching than usual, because it kept its eyes trained on the one-way mirror. Well, the one eye that could still see. The other had been cut in half, one half sat inside the socket while the other half was hanging outside of it. Blood stained its face, poured out of its neck where the collar sat, splattered across the floor where it sat on its knees. It bared its teeth at the electric current flowing through its neck, but that did nothing to stop the Handlers from increasing the power of the shock. The Executing Officer entered the room and slowly stepped towards the dog. He kept his rifle to his side. I watched the dog finally turn his head to look at the Executing officer. It shook its head.

“Please. I wanna go home.”

“It’ll be over soon, puppy.”

And the officer lifted his rifle. But before he could shoot, the dog was upon him. I watched in horror as it ripped out the muscles of the Executing Officer’s neck. It grabbed his rifle, shot at the chain tethering it to the wall, then shot at the one-way mirror into the room I was in. I ran as fast as I could, trying to find a weapon I was authorized to use. I was told that I would never have to deal with the dog, so I was never authorized to use Cytozine ammunition. My efforts fell short, because my vision went black. I knew this was the dog’s doing, and that my time was up. I didn’t even bother trying to feel my way around. It would find me before I would find my way out. So I sat down on the floor and waited for it to end.

I don’t know how much time passed, but eventually, I could see again, just a few feet around me. And, unfortunately, the dog was standing over me, grinning, showing its blood-stained teeth.

“P-Please don’t kill me.”

“...Or?”

“What…?”

“Or what? Don’t kill you, or what?”

“I—Please. I have a family—”

“Don’t care.”

“What do you mean you don’t care? You don’t care that you’re about to kill someone’s son?”

The dog slowly looked around us, though I couldn’t actually see what it was trying to gesture towards. No doubt, the countless dead bodies of my coworkers. I considered running, but I knew that would only make things more fun for the dog. So I tried begging again.

“Why are you doing this?”

“What do you mean?”

“Why are you killing us? Why are you killing me? I didn’t do anything to you!”

“Isn’t this what I’m supposed to do?”

I stopped before I said something stupid, because I knew I would. The dog technically wasn’t wrong, it was specifically converted to be a killing machine. In that moment, as I stared into the dog’s black eyes, I realized where I had gone wrong. The eyes of a rabid dog backed into a corner, of a scared boy burying dead rats, stared back at me. I started to apologize, but before I could continue pleading, K93-M52’s teeth were digging into my cheek, ripping the flesh from my cheek. It continued ravaging despite my cries to stop—down my face, across my jawline, further down. Its breath was hot on my neck. I could feel my fingers locking in the dog’s hair. I hadn’t noticed before how soft it was, how curly, jet black. Even as it pulled red meat out of my chest, I didn’t want to let go of it. In that moment, I wanted nothing more than to say its name. Not dog, not K9, I wanted it to be human. I wanted to ask, but found myself unable to. I did it the favor of pulling out it’s dangling eye, holding it as firmly as I could in my hand. It growled. From pain, not anger. I reveled in its humanity. This is exactly what I wanted. [DEMO] C-2234599 could never give me this. He would never be this close. With my last breath, I let go of its hair and let my hand drift down to its cheek.

“Thank you.”
