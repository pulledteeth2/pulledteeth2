decieving things including but not limited to: songs, windows, birds, and the dark

“Sometimes we have to be grateful for the small things,” they’d said, carving their pseudonym’s initials into the concrete steps with their switchblade. “At least I’m not you.”

The pigeon took no offence to it because it was, of course, just a bird and didn’t have the higher function to consider that Max had a better life than it. It was also dead, having flown into the floor-to-ceiling window that looked into the backstage area of the venue. Max noticed it as they were wandering around and went outside to look under the guise of a smoke break (that was more explainable to security than “I want to look at a dead bird”).

Max could feel the whining guitars and pulsing drums from the opening band in their sneakers even from outside. They actually really enjoyed the band, but being around the equipment for so long made their head hurt. Something about the static messing with their brainwaves. No one took it personally. Settling down on the steps, Max scooped the bird into their hands for closer observation. Yep. Definitely dead. They set it next to them, clear of the door’s path. Just in case.

So there they sat on that winter night: Max keeping the bird safe, and the bird keeping Max company. And as they thought of it more, were they really any better than the pigeon? Once domesticated, now doing their best to scrape by at the mercy of select kind-hearted people? Were they not just flying into extremely clean windows every night? Drawn in by presumed safety, only to pay for it later? Max envied the bird then. They picked the bird up again and walked away from the venue.

They navigated through smelly back alleys and skinny side streets until the sights were unfamiliar and the sounds weren’t ringing with hundreds of screams. They passed three 7-Eleven’s (all blasting Chopin) and a patrolling cop that was way too interested in where Max was going. The cruiser had slowed to a crawl beside Max long after they’d told the pig to fuck off. Just as Max turned into an alley, the car door opened and the officer began chasing them down. It was silly, really, for him to think he could’ve caught up with Max. Home advantage be damned. Max put miles between themself and the cop in a few minutes, and when the coast was clear, they stopped sprinting. The stone and brick buildings of the city had given way to… well, nothing. Nothing except for a grand metal gate topped with barbed wire. There was a sign on the gate, but Max couldn’t read it. It was in German. Maybe. Hopefully it wasn’t a warning sign, since they scaled the gate within two minutes. Would’ve been shorter if they hadn’t been carrying a dead bird and their shoe hadn’t got caught in the wire. They decided to leave it on the gate—I don’t need both shoes, they thought.

On the other side of the gate was an expansive forest that definitely wasn’t visible from outside. And it was strange, because these trees were hundreds of feet tall. Who would gate up a forest, anyway? Max turned back to face the gate, but it was now gone, replaced with more trees.

“Aw man,” they muttered. “I’m gonna be late.”

But it didn’t seem to matter too much to them, as they strolled quite casually through the woods. Seven crows circled above them, and one particularly large crow flew level with their head. The presence of the crows wouldn’t have bothered them if the flapping of their wings hadn’t matched a drum beat for ceremonial funeral rituals they heard in a Youtube video last year.

“Am I marked for death?” Max muttered to themself.

“No,” the large crow replied. “We’re just taking a walk. Isn’t that what you came here for?”

Max supposed this was true, but that didn’t answer why they were being sanctified. At the very least, they didn’t feel like they were in danger. Maybe they weren’t part of a funeral procession, and their crow entourage was actually looking out for Max. But for what? What could be so dangerous in a spontaneously generated forest in the middle of whatever city they were in today? Nothing Max couldn’t handle, that’s for sure.

The ground slowly started to incline, and Max hadn’t noticed until they were forced to climb, given how steep the area was. They were a decent climber, which made things a bit easier, but they were still only using one hand. The large crow, which Max decided would be called Dae, sometimes landed on their shoulders or their head and pulled them upwards. It was helping them climb, and Max was grateful even with the crow’s talons digging into their skin. It wasn’t the most painful thing they’d ever felt, and for that, they were even more grateful.

At the top of the hill was a body of water. Max didn’t know when large ponds became small lakes, and the distinction really didn’t matter much to them. Water was water, after all. They crawled to the edge, stopping just before falling in. The moon lit the clearing beautifully, so Max could see their shadowy reflection in the water. They tilted their head, and their shadow-self tilted its head in the opposite direction. Max smiled. They hoped that their shadow-self did too.

“It’s warm here,” Max said.

Dae was perched on their shoulder, shifting from foot to foot. “Heat rises, Max.”

“Sure, but it’s dramatically warmer. I put on a jacket earlier. Now I wish I didn’t have it.”

Dae said something else, but Max couldn’t hear. The crow flew off to join the others before they could ask for clarification. Now eight crows were circling and Max took that to be a bad omen. Seven was fine, but eight is just a bad number. But as they stared up at their corvid friends, they couldn’t bring themself to ask the birds to disperse. Besides, hadn’t Dae already told them they weren’t dying here? There was nothing to worry about.

They laid the pigeon they’d been carrying onto a burnt patch of grass. They figured they wouldn’t have enough time to find something to bury it in, so they just used their other shoe. The pigeon probably didn’t mind—it was dead, after all. And surely being buried in a beat-up Converse high top Max had been writing lyrics on for three years was better than being left for dead in a pile of cigarette butts. Max thought so, anyway. They clawed out a hole in the ground big enough for a size 10 shoe and gently set the improvised coffin inside.

“We’re gathered here today to commemorate the life of one mighty pigeon,” Max said solemnly, just as they learned to do when they were hosting funerals for street rats “I hadn’t known this creature in life, but I do know that it flies with grace in an afterlife free of deceiving windows. Amen.”

With formalities out of the way, they could properly lay their deceased companion to rest. The dirt clung to their hands this time even though it hadn’t when they dug the hole in the first place. Max was mildly annoyed, but thankfully they were kneeling right next to a sizable body of water.

It looked inviting. Max wasn’t sure if that was a valid word to describe water, but what better word was there when the ripples had formed to look like beckoning hands? “Swim with me, Max!” the water seemed to say. In Max’s head, of course. Talking water would be weird. So they took off their jacket and shorts (kept their socks on because “no socks is also kinda weird”), and slowly army crawled into the pond-lake/lake-pond. It was cold, but Max didn’t mind.

Another shadow figure swam beside them. It clearly wasn’t theirs. This was was much longer than Max’s shadow and didn’t have the same finesse that shadow-Max had. At first, Max was fine with it—I don’t own this water, I can’t tell anyone to leave—but they soon realized that the figure was getting closer. Max tried keeping some distance, but the thing just kept following them. Shit.

Max tried to stand, but there was something above them. Like a piece of ice or glass. It wasn’t lifting when they pushed against it. Stuck. They knew they couldn’t panic or they’d drown, so they kept swimming. Unfortunately, they weren’t as good of a swimmer as they were a runner or climber, and before they knew it, the thing had wrapped itself around Max’s ankle. Max tried convincing themself if was just a fish or something. There were no fish. Nothing in the water but Max and the shadow thing that was slowly inching up their body. They were pretty sure that it didn’t have a humanoid form earlier, but they could feel the distinctive shape of hands roaming over them. It hurt more than it should’ve. Hands didn’t usually hurt that bad, did they? Every night standing on the barrier, hands found their ways on Max’s legs, and their arms, and their shoulders. It wasn’t so bad, was it? They’ve known hands everywhere and it’s never been that bad. They closed their eyes and they were there: It was in Denver where some girl had scratched into the copperhead tattooed on their calf when they got too close to the edge of the stage. Or maybe it was when they’d been choked by a friend of a friend in LA. Or was it when they got jumped outside of their apartment in New York? Only God knew how many times Max had their hair pulled and in how many directions. Everyone’s hands blended together in a mass of “do normal people get touched this much?” and Max tried not to think about it too hard. Man up, Max.

Anyway, Max had a task at hand and Max thrived on tasks. Just not this time. Alarm bells were blaring in their waterlogged ears, pleading with them to get away from the shadow figure. Max tried again to swim up, but the glass covering the lake was still there. The shadow figure was enveloping them, and they were losing their vision. Water started filling their lungs with each desperate stroke. Further into the water, further into the hold of the creature then surrounding them. A hand cradled their head, gripping a fistful of hair and pulled back.

The moon was in fact just a streetlight. How long had Max been staring? Why had it taken so long for Max to realize why the light didn’t impress them like it usually did? Someone’s hand rested on their head. Max flinched at first, but settled when the hand raked through their hair. Max knew this hand. They looked up and saw one of their bandmates, Cameron. They smiled. He smiled back.

“Cold out here,” Cam said as he sat down next to Max. He brought out a hoodie for them, knowing they never wore one. It was one of his that he’d gotten when he played basketball in high school, but it may as well have been a communal jacket with how often it seemed to end up with Max’s things.

Max took the jacket but didn’t put it on at first. They could deal with the cold. And they’d be going back inside soon anyway, so what was the point? But Cam said it was cold, which means they should’ve been cold too. So they pulled the hoodie over their head.

The two of them sat in a comfortable silence. Max could feel Cameron shivering, so they wrapped their arms around him. They’d moved just enough that Cameron could see what was next to them.

“What’s that?”

Max glanced over and shrugged. “It’s a pigeon. He ran into the window, I think.”

Cam hummed in response, but didn’t say anything more. He didn’t need to. The opener was closing out their set, and soon, there would be no time for unrehearsed words. But for now, they were content to sit in the cold: Max keeping Cam warm, and Cam keeping Max company.
