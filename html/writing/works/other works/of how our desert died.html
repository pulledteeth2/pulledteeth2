Of How Our Desert Died

We were, at one time, a breathing Land. Our sand was stark against cyan skies. Orange, red, tan, brown. We were home to many. We were beautiful. We were whole. Our Mother, a great mountain, stood at our center. We are, and were, and always will be entitled to protect Her.

We saw the beetles make their homes, and with the beetles came the spiders. The spiders made their homes, and with the spiders came the lizards. The lizards made their homes, and with the lizards came the snakes. Then the coyotes.

And then there was Man.

Man failed at first. He didn’t know our heat, the glorious Sun, the rhythm of the life we give and take away. But Man believed He was the smartest. He gave Himself a crown, rode on the white horse and split the land as he wanted. Parts of us, He called Stands, or Castle, and He called His greatest point Valley City. The rest of us, what He deemed unimportant, was called The Wastes. He named our Mother Grand Mountain. As if He had the right. He told himself He was above the ways of the creatures that came before it. The coyotes and snakes and lizards and spiders and beetles were forced to retreat as Man made them to.

Man drew His mighty sword and the snakes were slain, for they had encroached too far into Man’s boundary. The coyotes lost some of their food, and the lizards were too many. Lizards, as all know, are only appetizing to snakes. The rhythm of our life was disturbed. We were stained with a fiery red that no rain could wash away.

Man could not live unpunished. The scales will balance again.

Our Mother sent forth a warning to Man: a black horse to each of His settlements. All three were slain by Man’s mighty sword. The Land retaliates promptly.

Mother sent Death onto Man’s homes. His livestock grew hungry and died. He claimed that sorcerers were sending Him ailments, that they rode ghastly horses and shot at Him with bows. He couldn’t perceive us as we were. He called us many names. He saw us as many things. We were many things.

We give and take away.

Cassius stood at his post in front of the Virgin Mary saloon. It was a quiet day, that one. Hot. Sunny. Maisie had come around like she always did, and Cassius yet again politely reminded her that he’s married with two children. “Jane don’t gotta know,” she whispered in his ear, swaying her hips just so as she walked past him, and he couldn’t tear away his gaze. He was, of course, just a man and all men are weak at some point in their lives. But Cassius wasn’t weak enough to ruin his life over a prostitute. And her face was ugly, in his opinion.

A stranger came by too. He had a lawman’s cap. Cassius wasn’t one to pry, but the man was the type to talk. So Cassius stood there and listened while the man—Otis, as Cassius learned—went on about the war being waged on the northern side of The Wastes. Castle was abandoned now, which Otis had seen from his patrols. “Terrible thing!” the man exclaimed. “Those damn mountain witches killin’ good people. Lotsa good folk travelin’ south to escape The Eclipse. You’re gonna get some interesting characters here in Valley City. Stay safe out here, son.”

Cassius tried not to worry about the land outside of Valley City. When he left his home in Stands ten years ago, he promised himself that he wouldn’t look back. And yet, with two weeks since the last letter from his mother, he wondered if he should’ve visited when the kids were born. But would his childhood home even look the same? Would the yellow-orange dirt blind him like it had when he was fourteen? Would his mother have fried snake bites cooling in the window, fussing over how much he’d changed since he left? Would she recognize him? What would he tell Jane if they went? “I ran away from home as a teenager because I couldn’t live on the land my father died on?” Would she be angry? Would she still love him? Or did it even matter at all? Had Stands disappeared in the black curtain of The Eclipse?

In his heatstroke ruminations, Cassius had accidentally let a gruff man through the door. Cassius couldn’t remember the man’s name, but knew that he’d been barred from entering before Cassius started working at Virgin Mary. He didn’t question it until the sound of shouting from inside interrupted his thoughts. The man was holding the bartender at gunpoint, and Cassius—ever reflexive—drew his own pistol and fired at the back of the man’s skull. The shot silenced the din of the room; the piano man stopped playing, poker players set down their cards, the whores didn’t dance. Eyes were on Cassius, who stood at the center. This was the worst part. The way townspeople looked at him like he was dangerous, and not just a man doing his job. They don’t look at lawmen like this, Cassius thought as he dragged the dead man out back. Old Silas would come by after closing and pick him up later for the pigs, just as he always did whenever someone died. And Cassius would be there to make small talk, feigning interest in how quickly 15 pigs eat a dead body. He wasn’t sure why Silas told him every time, but he didn’t have it in him to shut it down.

Otis had apparently been impressed by the display. He came back every day for the next four days, each time to compliment Cassius’ shot. “The war could use somebody like you,” he’d say in that crackling voice of his that started to annoy Cassius. “Those poor suckers in The Wastes might have a chance if they had you shootin’ for them.”

Cassius had never considered joining the militia. In fact, he wouldn’t have even been allowed to, had there not been a war. Not after being arrested at sixteen for drinking with that Castle outlaw. The man had found him wandering The Wastes alone, and took Cassius with him as he evaded lawmen. His three months with the man had many firsts for him; first time drinking, first time sleeping in The Wastes, first time seeing Grand Mountain up close. But it was that one night in Castle that ruined freedom for him. They both should’ve been arrested that night, but Cassius had lied and said he’d been the one to steal the whiskey. He stayed in Outer Depths Country Jail for eight months. The outlaw promised to visit—”We’ll leave The Wastes someday, kid”— but he never did. No one did. His mother didn’t even know he was locked up. When he got released, he had nowhere to go. Stuck drifting through The Wastes until he stopped in Valley City when he was eighteen.

“So? What d’ya think?” Otis’ gravelly voice pulled Cassius from his thoughts. He had been running on autopilot; Otis handed him the militia’s standard issued pump-action shotgun, and he aimed it at the sun. When was the last time he’d fired a shotgun? Hadn’t he been twelve, trying to scare off an outlaw from robbing his father? Hadn’t his finger locked around the trigger? Hadn’t he shot the wrong man?

...

The recoil hit him harder than expected. He’d shot plenty before, but standing under the eternally dark skies taking over The Wastes, he suddenly wasn’t the marksman his father trained him to be. The Valley City-based platoon took their place just south of Grand Mountain. Gunfire rained from both sides of the battle line. The militia was quickly dwindling, but the Mountain People stood strong.

The figures, Cassius realized, weren’t actually shooting at him. In fact, they didn’t have guns at all (or bows, as many in the militia seemed to think). Against orders, Cassius lowered his weapon. He watched the figures across the expanse of The Wastes. They weren’t attacking at all. No, they were dancing. Limbs loose, flailing, a kind of freedom Cassius didn’t know was possible.

The shotgun fell to his feet. He couldn’t stop staring, even as his fellow militia men were falling around him. Even as his general was screaming orders from the supposed safety of his horse. Cassius’ hands raised, absent of any conscious thought, and copied the flowing wave of the shadows.

Up, left, right, left, down, up, right.

His legs joined in the exuberant dance. He didn’t know what he was doing, but it was.. natural. Wrong, like singing a song he’d never heard, but knew the words to anyway. The Eclipse sang back to him, filling his ears with rhythmic chanting that he couldn’t understand. Over and over, entering his ears and filling his body with a weightless sensation he’d never felt before. It scared him. For the first time in a long time, he was truly scared. Scared as he was at fourteen when he left home. He looked again where Grand Mountain should’ve been, and saw The Eclipse still rushing towards his fleet. A large mass of darkness cascaded down the mountain, feeding the festival of the shadow witches on the ground. It had no eyes that Cassius could see, and yet, it still looked at him. It was all too real, just as real as he felt the ghost of hands settle on his cheeks. And he heard the voice of his mother say “Come and see”. He snapped out of his trance and ran. Ran for the horse of his general, who was now dead but still mounted. Cassius pushed the man away and rode the horse back home.

Cassius threw himself off of the horse when he got to Valley City’s square. The sun beat down on him worse than it had before, burning his hands as he scrabbled against the dusty ground. He should’ve stood, but he didn’t know if he could anymore.

“They’re coming!” he yelled, breathless, to any and every passerby. A woman stopped to help him to his feet, and in return, he grabbed her shoulders and shook her. “The Eclipse! It’s coming for Valley City!”

A small group had started gathering by that point, watching Cassius scream about the darkening of The Wastes, the blanket of shadow that had chased him back home. His wife, Jane, was there too. Tears stung her eyes, even as Cassius fell to his knees in front of her.

“Please, love,” he cried, forcing her hands to cup his face. He flinched. While he’d known this touch before, his face still burned where The Eclipse touched him. “You have to leave. Take Joshua and Faith somewhere safe. Past Valley City. Past The Wastes. Away from this wretched desert. Please.”

Jane stepped back. She couldn’t face him, but couldn’t look away either. The crowd surrounding them had grown substantially and Cassius watched, helpless, as lawmen’s fists rained down on him. His face was suddenly getting colder, and he realized he was crying. When was the last time he cried? When Faith was born, and before that when Joshua was born, but those were happy tears. Or was it fear? Before that, it had been when he was six, and his father shot that stray black horse that had wandered into Stands.

The bars of Cassius’ cell shook violently. He would’ve kept pretending to sleep if the voice that called out for him had been familiar. He didn’t move from his spot on the floor, but he lifted his head to see the stranger trying to get to him.

The man wore a militia coat, and his gun was still slung over his shoulder. His grin was way too wide for Outer Depths Country Jail. “Cassius! Hi!” he exclaimed, waving with both hands like a child. Like Faith did when Cassius came home from work every day. That was the only reason he got up, Cassius told himself. Because no stranger was ever this excited to see him.

“This isn’t a visiting area,” Cassius told the militia man. “Who are you? How did you get back here?”

The man’s smile faded. “Wh-What do you mean? It’s me. Callan? Callan Nichols?”

Cassius stared blankly at the man. Callan? He didn’t know a Callan. Or, at least, he didn’t think he knew a Callan. He stared at the man longer, trying to place him. There was something vaguely familiar about him. Maybe. The two men locked eyes, and that’s when it hit Cassius. Those eyes. That man. That Castle outlaw that had him arrested eight years ago. He stepped back from the bars like he’d been punched. In fact, he wished he had. Callan? Here? And not as a prisoner? Cassius never thought he’d see this man again. He didn’t want to see this man again. So he turned around and lied back on his spot on the floor. Callan had started talking again, but Cassius was already getting back to his nap.

The bars of his cell shook again, same as Callan’s voice. “I heard you joined the militia. They need you now, Cassius. No one’s ever seen The Eclipse and lived.”

Cassius wasn’t hearing it. He knew The Eclipse was moving fast. Saw it himself. Soon enough, Valley City wouldn’t be safe anymore; The Eclipse would swallow them too. He could only hope that Jane got the kids on their horse and rode out before it caught them. Callan must’ve heard his thoughts, because he shook the bars a third time. “The Eclipse is a week away. You have to rejoin the war.”

This got Cassius’ attention. Rejoin the war? For what? Those Mountain People hadn’t hurt him, had they? He knew it was killing everyone, but it was, in his eyes, the most he’d ever felt before. Why would he fight it? All for a city that wouldn’t protect his family. How long had lawmen known about The Eclipse before it reached Castle? They hadn’t warned anyone. Certainly not his mother, who probably hadn’t gotten out of Stands fast enough. Certainly not now, as he sat in this jail cell. A week. Barely enough time to get to Castle, let alone past The Wastes. Even if Jane and the kids left now, they’d be lost to The Eclipse. They all would. Why would they fight it?

Callan had apparently still been talking. “Remember that dust storm?” and “I loved watching the stars through your eyes” and “I don’t want to see it go, Cassius.” As if any of them would see it. No one had seen what Cassius saw on the battlefield. The shadows dancing? The voice of his mother? The overwhelming fear he’d never felt before? Why would he fight?

So he didn’t. Said nothing when Callan shot the door open, and subsequently shot the guard that tried to stop him. Agreed to return to The Wastes, but only if he could go home first.

Callan’s horse, Bessie, only took them so far. She also bit Cassius when he tried to mount, so he had to walk. Stupid horse, Cassius thought as Grand Mountain came into view. Callan was a ways behind, having spent too much time trying to pull Bessie along. In the end, he left her in The Wastes and hoped she would survive on her own. Cassius knew better.

“There’s no witches,” Callan noticed when they reached the base of the mountain. “Do you think they’ve all left for the towns?”

Cassius didn’t say anything. Not because he believed they were gone, but because his answer didn’t matter.

“Well, since you’re The Eclipse expert, you can go plead with the witch leader up there. I’ll stay here in case more show up.”

Cassius didn’t bother arguing or explaining why it was a terrible plan. He, in fact, didn’t care. What did it matter that he was the first to climb Grand Mountain? It wouldn’t save Joshua and Faith. It wouldn’t save Jane. It wouldn’t save his poor mother. He wouldn’t save anyone. Not the people in Castle, not the people in Stands, not Callan. Not himself.

The wind whipped sand into Cassius’ eyes as he made his way up Grand Mountain. For a moment, he thought of Joshua’s first dust storm. Three years ago, they were out at Silas’ stable where they kept Sasha, their horse. His boy had cried in his arms because the sand hurt. Helpless, all Cassius could do was tie a bandana around Joshua’s face and carry him home as quickly as his legs could take him. He couldn’t breathe the sand out of Joshua’s lungs. Couldn’t curse God to stop choking his son. Like he couldn’t beg Him now to let his family live. Not that God ever answered him anyway.

He decided to stop halfway up the mountain. His legs hurt from the four days of travel, and he doubted there was anything at the top anyway. Besides, there was a cave here that was calling to him.

“Come closer, Cassius,” he heard in his mother’s voice. “You’ve gotten so tall.”

He crawled through the tunnels of the cave, but it was difficult to tell where his mother’s voice was coming from. She weaved into a larger rope of voices he didn’t know and animal sounds he’d heard his whole life. They reverberated off of the walls, looping muddled feedback through his ears. It hurt, but he still kept crawling.

The tunnels converged into a single cavern, where a dark cloud pulsed in the center. Every voice spoke to him, not through his ears but directly into his head. “Cassius,” they all said to him. “You’re here.”

He was. Here, in this dark cave with all that had surrendered to The Eclipse. Here, with Jane’s voice in his ear. Here, with the shadows of Joshua and Faith playing at his feet. He closed his eyes. How badly he wanted to hold them again. He wanted to hold Faith’s hand like he always did when she got excited about a game. He wanted to dangle Josh upside down. Hear their laughs. Wipe their tears. Apologize for not saving them. He wanted Jane to kiss him so softly and tell him that he was okay. They were okay. Alive and healthy and happy.

When he opened his eyes, he saw them. Jane, Joshua, and Faith, standing bright in front of a backdrop of nothing. Fath ran at him first, headbutting him in the knee. Usually, it annoyed him, but now it just made him cry. Cassius picked up his daughter, then his son, and he hugged his wife.

Finally, he was home.
